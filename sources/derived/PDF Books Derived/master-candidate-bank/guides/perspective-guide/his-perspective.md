# His Perspective

Sometimes he is not trying to dismiss, control, or abandon. Sometimes he is overwhelmed, trying to fix it fast, or trying not to make things worse — and landing badly anyway.

## Best used when…

- he often says “I’m trying to help” and she says “you’re not hearing me”
- he shuts down fast when he feels criticised
- he reaches for solutions, logic, or space when she wants presence
- both of you feel confused about how the same moment can feel so different from each side

## What it can feel like from his side

It can sound like this internally:

- “I’m already failing.”
- “If I say the wrong thing this gets worse.”
- “I need a minute.”
- “I’m trying to solve it.”
- “Nothing I say works.”

That does not make the impact okay.  
But it does matter, because repair gets easier once the panic/defence pattern is named.

## What it often looks like from her side

What she may experience:

- he goes cold when she is hurting
- he explains instead of listening
- he disappears into silence
- he treats her feelings like a problem to fix
- he seems more interested in ending the moment than understanding it

So even when he thinks he is de-escalating, she may experience distance.

## What usually goes wrong

He may:

- hear protest as attack
- defend before understanding
- try to solve the feeling instead of staying with it
- take a break without clearly returning
- assume every complaint means he is a bad partner

Once that happens, the conversation is no longer about the issue.  
It becomes about getting through to each other at all.

## Do This First

If you are him, try this sequence:

1. notice when you are flooding  
2. slow your body down before you argue your case  
3. ask what hurts before you explain yourself  
4. only take a break if you name your return  

A useful line:

> I can feel myself getting defensive.  
> I want to understand before I explain myself.

## Say This

Try one of these:

> What do you need from me right now?

> What are you most concerned about here?

> What are you feeling that I’m not getting yet?

Or:

> I think I’m hearing the complaint and missing the pain.  
> Help me understand what this has been like for you.

## If you are reading this as her

This guide is not asking you to parent him, excuse him, or reduce your standards.

It is simply saying:

- his withdrawal may be overwhelm, not indifference
- his fixing may be panic, not carelessness
- the pattern improves faster when the overwhelm is named instead of guessed at

You still get to require:
- return after a pause
- nondefensive listening
- accountability
- better behaviour over time

## Why this matters

Small repeated misses create distrust fast.

Not just affairs or giant betrayals.  
Ordinary moments of:
- not turning toward
- not staying present
- not coming back
- not hearing the hurt

Those are what make a partner start feeling alone.

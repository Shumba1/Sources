# We Keep Fighting

If you keep having the same argument, the problem usually is not just the topic.

It is the cycle the two of you enter when one or both of you feels hurt, unseen, criticised, or unsafe.

## Best used when…

- You keep having the same fight with different wording.
- One of you pushes, protests, or escalates while the other defends, shuts down, or leaves.
- The issue seems to “end,” but never really gets resolved.
- After a fight, both of you feel lonely, misunderstood, or emotionally far apart.

## Not for…

- abuse, intimidation, coercion, or fear
- violence or threats
- active affairs or repeated betrayal that is still ongoing
- addiction-driven chaos that is swallowing all accountability
- situations where one partner uses “repair language” to avoid responsibility

## What’s Happening

The visible argument is often about something real:

- money
- time
- parenting
- chores
- sex
- in-laws
- priorities

But the deeper pain is usually about what the moment seems to mean:

- Do I matter to you?
- Do you respect me?
- Do you understand me?
- Can I reach you when I need you?
- Am I failing you?
- Am I alone with this again?

That is why the same fight feels so loaded.  
The issue is real, but the cycle around the issue is what keeps hurting the bond.

## What Makes It Worse

These moves usually deepen the cycle:

- criticism
- counterattacking
- trying to prove who is right
- invalidating the other person’s feelings
- shutting down without any bridge back
- forcing problem-solving before either person feels understood
- treating the partner like the enemy instead of treating the pattern like the enemy

The more you repeat the cycle, the more both of you start expecting it.

## Do This First

Name the pattern before you try to solve the topic.

Say, out loud if you can:

> I think we are in our cycle again.

Then stop trying to win the point for one moment and ask:

- What just got triggered in me?
- What am I assuming this means?
- What do I need right now to feel close or safe?
- What might be happening inside my partner that I cannot see yet?

You do not need the perfect answer.  
You need enough awareness to stop the usual next move.

## Say This

> I do not think you are the enemy right now.  
> I think we are stuck in our pattern.  
> Underneath my reaction, I am feeling hurt and protective.  
> I want to slow this down instead of making it worse.

Or:

> I know the topic matters, but I do not think we can solve it well while we both feel this activated.  
> I want to understand what is happening in us first.

## Do This Tonight

Do one short interruption-and-repair attempt.

Use this sequence:

1. Name the cycle.
2. Name your own deeper feeling without accusation.
3. Validate one thing that makes sense about your partner’s reaction.
4. Delay full problem-solving until both of you are steadier.

Example:

> When this happens, I get scared that I do not matter and I start pushing.  
> I can see how that makes it hard for you to stay open.  
> I do not want to keep doing this the same way.

Or:

> When I feel criticised, I get defensive fast and then I go away from you.  
> I can see how that leaves you alone with the whole problem.  
> I want to try this differently.

## 7-Day Reset

### Day 1
Each of you write down your usual trigger, your usual meaning, and your usual reaction.

### Day 2
Learn one sentence that interrupts your cycle before it gets hot.

### Day 3
Practise one softer startup for a minor complaint.

### Day 4
When tension rises, take a brief break with a clear return time instead of disappearing.

### Day 5
Make one repair after a miss, even if the miss felt small.

### Day 6
Ask each other: “What usually happens inside you before the fight gets big?”

### Day 7
Revisit one recurring issue and talk about the pattern around it, not just the content of it.

## Escalate If…

Use a stronger path or outside help if:

- arguments include fear, threats, intimidation, or coercion
- contempt is becoming normal
- one partner refuses any accountability at all
- addiction, trauma, or betrayal is overwhelming the conversation
- every attempt to interrupt the cycle gets turned into a new attack

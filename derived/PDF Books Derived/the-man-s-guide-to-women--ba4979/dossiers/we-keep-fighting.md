## 2. Target fit summary

This is the strongest destination in the source.

The source is unusually practical when it explains that many fights are not really about facts or solutions. They are about a repeated pattern: protest, defensiveness, flooding, shutdown, and unresolved hurt. That maps cleanly to MarriageOS V1.

This dossier is valuable because it names the common male-side failure pattern without demonising men. It also gives MarriageOS a sharp intervention set: downregulate first, hear the pain under the protest, ask direct questions, and stop treating every complaint as an attack.

It should not become a gender-war piece. It also should not frame women as irrational or men as emotionally defective. The pattern is usable; the ideology is not.

## 5. Core content logic

- problem pattern: One partner raises an issue to feel heard or to change something painful. The other hears attack, floods, gets defensive, stonewalls, or tries to solve too fast. The first partner escalates because they still do not feel heard.
- likely user state/substate: “Every conversation becomes a fight”; “I can’t say anything without them shutting down”; “I try to help and it somehow gets worse”; “We end up arguing about the argument.”
- what is happening: The couple is confusing protest with attack and confusion with bad intent. Flooding then kills listening, humour, and empathy.
- what makes it worse: trying to win; solving before understanding; counter-criticism; walking off without a real break; contempt; global statements like “you always” and “you never.”
- first corrective principle: regulate before resolution.
- first behaviour move: stop the escalation, name flooding, ask what hurts underneath the protest.
- script logic: “I think we are getting into our old loop. I’m starting to defend instead of listen. Slow down and tell me what hurts here.”
- tonight move: one paused, nondefensive conversation using the three questions: What do you need? What are you concerned about? What are you feeling?
- reset logic: reduce heat, increase turn-toward behaviour, replace rebuttal with curiosity.
- escalation boundary: if anger becomes intimidation, contempt becomes chronic, or there is no safe way to pause and re-enter, the module must branch outward.
- related guide(s): guides/his-perspective; guides/read-both
- related product(s): products/hard-conversation-scripts-pack

## 6. Claims to preserve

- Many fights are really failed bids for understanding.
- Defensiveness, contempt, criticism, and stonewalling accelerate damage.
- Flooding is a real bodily state that kills listening.
- A real pause is better than escalating through it.
- Asking about needs, concerns, and feelings is often more useful than arguing facts.
- Trust erodes through repeated turning away.

## 7. Claims to soften or translate

### Item 1
- original idea: Men flood; women stay calmer and want to talk.
- why it cannot be carried forward verbatim: Too categorical and too easy to turn into doctrine.
- how it should be translated into MarriageOS language: in many protest-withdraw dynamics, one partner floods and shuts down faster while the other pursues harder to get through.

### Item 2
- original idea: Women are angry for specific female reasons.
- why it cannot be carried forward verbatim: Overgeneralised and gender-essentialist.
- how it should be translated into MarriageOS language: repeated protest often signals pain around unfairness, helplessness, exclusion, or lack of accountability.

### Item 3
- original idea: The man is the crucial variable in whether the relationship succeeds.
- why it cannot be carried forward verbatim: Too absolute for MarriageOS doctrine.
- how it should be translated into MarriageOS language: in many distressed pairs, one partner’s defensive pattern is carrying a lot of the escalation load and must be interrupted.

## 2. Target fit summary

This is one of the source's most justified downstream targets.

The book is overtly directed at men, and its best material is not broad truth about women but male-side coaching about what tends to go wrong: hearing attack where there is protest, fixing where there is a bid for connection, and withdrawing when the bond actually needs presence.

That is excellent guide material when rewritten without the hero/zero rhetoric, without shame, and without treating men as defective or women as inscrutable.

## 5. Core content logic

- problem pattern: He often feels suddenly accused, controlled, or set up to fail. He tries to calm things down by solving, explaining, leaving, or going quiet. It lands as disinterest or abandonment.
- likely user state/substate: “I’m trying to help”; “I don’t know what the right answer is”; “I need a minute but it looks like I don’t care”; “Nothing I say seems to work.”
- what is happening: he may be responding to overwhelm instead of to the relationship need under the conversation.
- what makes it worse: arguing facts too early; telling her not to feel that way; explaining instead of listening; pausing without a real return; assuming every complaint means failure.
- first corrective principle: you do not have to solve the feeling first; you have to stay with it.
- first behaviour move: notice flooding, slow down, and ask what hurts.
- script logic: “I want to understand before I defend myself. Tell me what this has been like for you.”
- related repair links: repair/we-keep-fighting; repair/pressure-loop

## 6. Claims to preserve

- Many men try to help by solving too quickly.
- Flooding makes listening harder.
- A pause can be helpful if it is real, named, and followed by return.
- Listening and curiosity are often more useful than explanation.
- Small repeated misses erode trust.

- how it should be translated into MarriageOS language: some people default to solving, some to emotional processing, and those defaults can clash badly under stress.

# We Keep Fighting

When one partner protests to be heard and the other hears attack, the conversation stops being about the issue and starts being about survival.

## Best used when…

- the same argument keeps repeating in slightly different forms
- one of you feels unheard and protests harder
- the other feels blamed, floods, and either defends or shuts down
- you keep ending up in criticism, contempt, defensiveness, or silence

## Not for…

- intimidation, threats, or fear
- physical aggression
- chronic contempt that makes repair bids unsafe
- situations where one partner refuses any re-entry after a pause

## What’s Happening

This pattern often looks like:

- one person raises an issue because something hurts
- the other hears attack instead of pain
- they defend, explain, or go quiet
- the first person escalates because they still do not feel heard
- both leave the conversation more alone than before

The surface topic can be chores, tone, sex, timing, money, or parenting.

The real fight is usually about something under the topic:
- “Do I matter here?”
- “Will you be there for me?”
- “Can I get through to you?”
- “Are we on the same team?”

## What Makes It Worse

These moves nearly always deepen the loop:

- solving before understanding
- arguing facts while the hurt is still hot
- saying “you’re overreacting” or “that’s not what happened”
- counterattacking
- going silent without a real pause-and-return plan
- global language like “you always” and “you never”
- letting contempt into the room

## Do This First

**Regulate before you resolve.**

If you can feel yourself speeding up, tightening, or getting ready to win, stop.

Use this sequence:

1. say you need to slow the conversation down  
2. take a real pause if needed  
3. come back and ask what hurts underneath the complaint  

A useful line:

> I can feel myself getting defensive.  
> I do want to hear this properly.  
> Slow down and tell me what hurts most here.

## Say This

Use one or more of these three questions:

- What do you need?
- What are you concerned about?
- What are you feeling?

Or:

> I think I’m hearing the protest but missing the pain.  
> Help me understand what this has been like for you.

## Do This Tonight

Set aside **15 to 20 minutes**.

Rules:
- no scorekeeping
- no bringing in five old fights
- no fact-check war
- no leaving without naming a return time

Each person answers:

- What hurt me here?
- What did I need that I did not know how to say clearly?
- What did I do that made this harder?

End with one next-time change from each side.

## 7-Day Reset

### Day 1
Notice your fight pattern and name it out loud when it starts.

### Day 2
Replace one defensive explanation with one curiosity question.

### Day 3
If either of you floods, take a real break and return when you said you would.

### Day 4
Ask each other: “What do you need from me when you’re upset with me?”

### Day 5
Catch one “you always” or “you never” before it leaves your mouth.

### Day 6
Offer one repair move after tension: a softer tone, a touch, an apology, or a return question.

### Day 7
Review the week and name the one shift that reduced heat the most.

## Escalate If…

Move to a stronger repair path if:

- breaks become abandonment
- anger becomes intimidation
- contempt is common
- one person cannot listen without immediate counterattack
- you keep circling the same wound with no increase in safety

## 2. Target fit summary

This source is stronger for gridlock than it first appears.  
Its distinctive contribution is that repetitive fights are often not just about bad communication; they are about dodged choices, fused identities, and hidden punishment.  
That makes it a useful antidote to shallow "just communicate better" advice.  
The final module should not be jargon-heavy, cynical, or contemptuous.  
It should name the structure of the fight clearly and give the user one steadier next move.

## 5. Core content logic

- problem pattern: The couple keeps returning to the same fight. One pushes, one resists, both feel trapped, and the argument never really closes.
- likely user state/substate: "We have this fight in different clothes"; "Nothing gets solved"; "I feel like I either surrender or explode."
- what is happening: A real choice is being dodged. Both people want relief without loss, so the conflict keeps circling. Along the way, passive cruelty and control moves seep in.
- what makes it worse: demanding two choices for yourself and none for the other person; sarcasm; punish-withdraw patterns; trying to win rather than choose; confusing comfort with health.
- first corrective principle: identify the real dilemma before you argue another lap around it.
- first behaviour move: calm yourself enough to name the one thing you do not want to choose between.
- script logic: support language like "I think we're dodging a real choice and turning it into a recurring fight."
- tonight move: one short conversation focused only on naming the real dilemma, not solving the whole marriage.
- reset logic: reduce reactive cruelty, stop stealing the partner's choice, and practise one steadier response when the usual fight begins.
- escalation boundary: if contempt, intimidation, or fear dominates the exchange, the module must branch to stronger support.

- related repair(s): repair/post-fight-reset

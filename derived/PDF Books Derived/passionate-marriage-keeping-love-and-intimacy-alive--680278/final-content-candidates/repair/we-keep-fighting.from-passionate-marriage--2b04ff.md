# We Keep Fighting

When the same fight keeps returning, the issue is often not just bad communication.  
Sometimes you are circling a real choice neither of you wants to face.

## Best used when…

- you keep having the same argument in different clothes
- one of you pushes and the other resists, shuts down, or punishes sideways
- apologies happen, but the structure of the fight does not change
- you feel stuck between surrender and explosion
- the relationship has become tense, brittle, or quietly cruel

## Not for…

- violence, intimidation, or real fear
- chronic contempt that makes calm repair impossible without outside help
- situations where one person has no real voice or no room to disagree safely

## What’s Happening

Some fights stay alive because there is a real dilemma underneath them.

It often looks like this:

- both people want relief
- neither wants the loss attached to the real choice
- so the choice gets dodged
- the fight becomes repetitive
- passive cruelty, sarcasm, or punishment starts leaking in

That is why some arguments never really end.  
They are not being solved because the actual dilemma is not being named.

## What Makes It Worse

These moves usually deepen the pattern:

- wanting two choices for yourself and none for the other person
- punishing your partner for having a real preference
- acting as if everyday cruelty “does not count”
- using withdrawal, tone, delay, or contempt as side-channel punishment
- confusing a stale truce with real peace
- trying to win the argument instead of face the choice

A lot of couples are not really fighting about the topic in front of them.  
They are fighting about what they do not want to choose between.

## Do This First

**Calm down enough to name the real dilemma.**

Ask yourself:

> What am I actually trying not to choose between?

Examples:

- closeness vs freedom
- honesty vs approval
- spending vs saving
- comfort vs growth
- sex vs safety
- family peace vs real boundaries

You do not need the whole solution first.  
You need the real problem named cleanly.

## Say This

> I think we keep turning a real dilemma into a recurring fight.  
> I do not want another lap around the same argument.  
> I want us to name what we are actually trying not to choose between.

Or:

> I think we are both trying to avoid loss, and it is making us hard on each other.

## Do This Tonight

Have one **15-minute conversation** with one purpose only:

**Name the underlying dilemma.**

Rules:

- no solving the whole issue tonight
- no relitigating every old example
- no character attacks
- no sarcasm
- each person gets one uninterrupted turn

Use this sentence stem:

> The part I least want to admit is that I feel forced to choose between ______ and ______.

That is enough for one night.

## 7-Day Reset

### Day 1
Name the recurring fight and the hidden dilemma underneath it.

### Day 2
Catch one passive punishment move:
- silence used as punishment
- sharp tone
- sarcastic joke
- late compliance
- “forgetting”
- cold distance

### Day 3
Replace one punishment move with a clean statement:
> I am upset and need a pause. I do not want to punish you sideways.

### Day 4
Say out loud what loss you are trying to avoid.

### Day 5
Ask your partner:
> What choice feels most costly to you here?

### Day 6
Notice whether you are trying to force your partner to make your choice easier instead of making your own more honestly.

### Day 7
Review:
- What is the real dilemma?
- What punishment pattern showed up most?
- What helped us stay more adult?

## Escalate If…

Open a stronger support path if:

- the fight includes contempt or fear most of the time
- one person has to disappear to keep the peace
- every disagreement becomes moral indictment
- passive aggression is so entrenched that honest speech cannot hold
- there is no safe room for different preferences

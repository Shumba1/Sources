# Secure Love — Source Normaliser Bundle

## 1. Source registration summary

- source_id: SRC-SL-001
- source_key: secure-love--ef2040
- source_title: *Secure Love*
- author: Julie Menanno
- source_type: book-pdf
- overall usefulness: high
- MarriageOS suitability: high with filtering
- dominant mode: attachment-framed couples therapy
- caution level: medium

### Why this source matters

This is one of the cleanest source books in the current batch for translating
relationship pain into an operational model instead of vague encouragement.
Its biggest contribution is not a new set of rituals. It is a framework for
understanding why couples keep missing each other, why repeated conflict
feels so destabilising, and why surface solutions fail when attachment safety is
damaged.

### Main risks

- therapy terminology can become bloated or over-clinical in product copy
- users may start weaponising labels like anxious / avoidant / disorganized
- some claims are framed as near-universal when MarriageOS should preserve
  room for nuance
- source must not be used to normalise unsafe relationships; the book itself
  explicitly excludes abusive dynamics

## 2. Theme clusters

### Cluster A — attachment as the hidden layer beneath recurring conflict
Key idea:
Most couple fights are not fundamentally about the surface issue. They are
about unmet attachment needs, threat, protective stances, and repeated
rupture.

MarriageOS value:
Excellent backbone for **we-keep-fighting** and adjacent conflict modules.

### Cluster B — secure attachment is a felt experience, not a slogan
Key idea:
Healthy relationship security is experienced in the nervous system as warmth,
soothing, confidence, and reliable responsiveness.

MarriageOS value:
Strong explanatory material for why users may “know” their partner loves them
but still not *feel* secure.

### Cluster C — attachment needs can be named explicitly
Key idea:
Closeness becomes clearer when translated into “to feel close to you, I need…”
language.

MarriageOS value:
Very strong script feeder for hard conversations, repair, and emotional
translation.

### Cluster D — attachment styles as adaptations
Key idea:
Anxious, avoidant, and disorganized patterns are adaptive childhood strategies
that now distort adult connection.

MarriageOS value:
Useful as interpretation logic, but needs tone control. MarriageOS should use
style language as clarification, not as identity branding.

### Cluster E — the negative cycle is the enemy
Key idea:
The partner is not the enemy. The couple’s negative communication cycle is.

MarriageOS value:
This is one of the highest-value lines in the entire source and ports directly into
repair UX.

### Cluster F — rupture repair requires environment, not just apology
Key idea:
Repairs work only inside an attachment-friendly environment shaped by
validation, regulation, and reduced shame.

MarriageOS value:
Excellent input for repair modules and future repair scripts products.

### Cluster G — sex is bond-sensitive
Key idea:
Sex is not a separate silo. The attachment climate of the relationship heavily
shapes desire, rejection, pursuit, withdrawal, and repair.

MarriageOS value:
Strong fit for **pressure-loop** and desire-related content.

### Cluster H — workable intruders
Key idea:
Depression, trauma, and addiction complicate repair but do not erase the need
for attachment safety; some issues require outside treatment.

MarriageOS value:
Critical boundary logic. Good for escalation rules and triage language.

## 3. Atomic extract cards

### Keep
- unmet attachment needs beneath conflict
- secure attachment as felt safety
- attachment needs list
- four Cs: comfort / connection / cooperation / conflict
- anxious / avoidant pair patterning
- anatomy of a trigger
- partner is not the enemy / cycle is
- attachment-friendly environment
- repair and rupture logic
- sex as bond-sensitive, especially pursue / withdraw dynamics
- addiction / trauma as workable intruders
- abuse exclusion

### Keep but translate
- attachment-style labels
- “vulnerability heals” language
- therapy-room phrasing around “buy the reframe”
- childhood explanatory stories
- self-growth / self-parenting ideas

### Ignore or down-rank
- heavy attachment-theory history detail
- meta-explanations of the therapist’s professional journey
- long case storytelling where the mechanism can be stated more efficiently
- index-only material or repeated recaps

### Caution
- do not over-explain every disagreement as attachment
- do not imply insecure attachment is destiny
- do not use labels to sort men and women into simplistic buckets
- do not let this source overpower clear moral lines around abuse, coercion, addiction, or betrayal

## 5. Dossier recommendations

### 1. we-keep-fighting
Why:
This source’s single strongest conversion is repeated conflict driven by unmet
attachment needs and mutual protective stances.

### 2. quiet-drift
Why:
The source explicitly names chronic coexistence, unrepaired ruptures, and
distance that grows when couples stop repairing.

### 3. pressure-loop
Why:
The book’s sex chapter is strongest when treated as attachment-driven pursue /
withdraw and emotional climate, not as generic libido advice.

## 6. Conversion rules for final content

- keep the language adult, clean, and practical
- reduce attachment jargon by 30–40%
- use labels sparingly and only when they reduce shame or confusion
- always preserve the abuse boundary
- never let “understanding why” become “excusing harmful behaviour”
- convert theory into sequences, scripts, and observable patterns
- keep the partner/cycle distinction explicit

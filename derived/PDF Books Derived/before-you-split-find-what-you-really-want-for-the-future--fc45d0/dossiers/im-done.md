## 2. Target fit summary

This source is exceptionally strong for the moment when a spouse feels done, trapped, or close to bolting.  
Its best contribution is not “stay at all costs.” Its best contribution is the diagnostic frame: unhappy vs harmful, split vs survive vs save, and the warning that pain can distort long-term decision quality.  
The final module should not moralise, sermonise, or guilt users into staying.  
It should lower panic, restore discrimination, and direct the user toward the correct lane: safety, temporary stabilisation, or repair.

## 5. Core content logic

- problem pattern: The user feels finished, cornered, or emotionally depleted and wants an answer now.
- likely user state/substate: “I cannot do this anymore”; “I want out”; “I do not know if this is temporary or terminal.”
- what is happening: Pain is compressing the horizon. The user may be conflating unsafe with unbearable, or surviving with repairing.
- what makes it worse: making the entire future decision during peak activation; using one fight as the whole verdict; confusing temporary relief with wise direction; talking only to biased voices.
- first corrective principle: sort for safety first, then slow the decision.
- first behaviour move: define which lane this actually is — harmful, unhappy-but-activated, or surviving-and-numb.
- script logic: support language like “I may be done with this version of us, but I do not have to decide everything tonight.”
- reset logic: seventy-two hours of no irreversible decision, one outside support contact, one honest classification of the state, one clear next step.
- escalation boundary: any violence, coercion, real fear, or objective harm exits this module into safety planning.

## 6. Claims to preserve

- harmful and unhappy are not the same
- emotional overload can distort decisions
- survival mode is not the same as repair
- slowing a decision is often wiser than forcing one
- outside perspective can stabilise distorted narratives

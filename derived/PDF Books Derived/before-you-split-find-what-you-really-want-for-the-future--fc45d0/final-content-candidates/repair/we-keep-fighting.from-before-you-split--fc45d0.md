# We Keep Fighting

When the same fight keeps returning, the issue is usually bigger than the topic. The topic matters. But the pattern is doing most of the damage.

## Best used when…

- The same argument keeps coming back in slightly different clothes.
- One or both of you feel blamed, unheard, or chronically misread.
- You keep leaving fights more injured than informed.
- You both feel like the other person is the real problem.

## Not for…

- coercion, fear, humiliation, or threat
- contempt so strong that basic emotional safety is gone
- situations where one partner is using “communication work” to avoid accountability for harmful behaviour

## What’s Happening

Couples usually think they are fighting about the thing:

- lateness
- money
- chores
- sex
- work
- parenting
- in-laws

Often they are also fighting through:

- hidden expectations
- old trigger stories
- blame language
- body-level escalation
- one-dimensional victim narratives

That is why the same fight feels so repetitive.  
The issue is not only the topic.  
It is the pattern running the topic.

## What Makes It Worse

These moves usually deepen the loop:

- “you always” / “you never”
- trying to win before you understand
- jumping straight to logic, fixing, or proof
- treating your partner’s weakness as the whole story of who they are
- staying in the conversation after you have already hit red-zone physiology
- assuming only one person is the victim every time

## Do This First

**Get out of the red zone before you try to solve content.**

If your body is already hot, flooded, shaky, numb, or racing, you are too activated to do high-quality repair.

Think in three zones:

- **green** — calm enough to talk
- **yellow** — getting activated, still recoverable
- **red** — flooded, sharp, unsafe for good speech

If you are in red:
- stop
- breathe
- pause the conversation
- come back when both of you are lower-threat again

## Say This

> We are not only fighting about the thing. We are fighting through blame, assumptions, and old stories.

Or:

> I want to understand what this means to you before I argue my case.

Or, when you notice escalation:

> I am going red. I do not want to damage this further. Let’s pause and come back.

## Do This Tonight

Have one short conversation with these rules:

- no “always” and no “never”
- no character assassination
- one person speaks, the other reflects
- validation before solution
- curiosity before case-making

Try this sequence:

1. **Name the topic**  
   “We’re fighting about money / time / chores / sex / parenting.”

2. **Name the pattern**  
   “We also keep slipping into blame and defence.”

3. **Name the meaning**  
   “This is what the issue means to me.”

4. **Validate before replying**  
   “I can see why that lands hard for you.”

5. **Ask one question**  
   “What are you afraid happens if this keeps going?”

## 7-Day Reset

### Day 1
Catch and remove one blame phrase before it lands.

### Day 2
Notice your body earlier. Name green, yellow, or red.

### Day 3
Validate one emotion before offering any solution.

### Day 4
Ask one curiosity question instead of assuming motive.

### Day 5
Name one unrealistic or unspoken expectation driving the argument.

### Day 6
Replace “me versus you” with “what helps us?”

### Day 7
Review one recent fight and ask:
- what was the topic?
- what was the pattern?
- what made it worse?
- what helped even a little?

## Escalate If…

Move to a stronger support path if:

- you cannot stay out of contempt
- one person always shuts the conversation down through dominance or fear
- every pause becomes avoidance instead of repair
- the fights are affecting your children or your basic sense of safety

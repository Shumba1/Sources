# Dossier: attachment-patterns

## Proposed MarriageOS target
- **Slug:** attachment-patterns
- **Type:** knowledge
- **Status:** strong fit

## Why this target
Chapter 12 offers a clean, durable conceptual frame for understanding why two people can want connection and still collide. It is the strongest knowledge fit in Part 4 because it explains protective patterns without requiring pathologising language.

## Core source signals
- Secure attachment functions as both **safe haven** and **secure base**.
- Adult attachment variation is commonly organised around **anxiety** and **avoidance**.
- Anxiety tends toward **hyperactivating** strategies: protest, pursuing, clinging, control, reassurance-seeking.
- Avoidance tends toward **deactivating** strategies: distance, suppression, emotional self-containment, minimising needs.
- Insecurity is linked with less constructive conflict, more exit/neglect tendencies, and more negative interpretations.

## MarriageOS translation
This should become a piece about **patterns under stress**, not personality verdicts.

Recommended framing:
- “When closeness feels unsafe, people protect differently.”
- “Some people protect by pursuing harder. Others protect by withdrawing harder.”
- “Neither pattern is proof of bad character. Both usually reflect protection.”

## What to keep
- Safe haven / secure base distinction
- Anxiety vs avoidance as orthogonal dimensions
- Protest vs shutdown language
- The idea that self-views, other-views, and attention biases shape behaviour

## What to remove or soften
- Clinical tone
- Trait essentialism
- Overuse of attachment jargon
- Any implication that attachment style fully explains a marriage

## Recommended final shape
A knowledge article with:
1. a simple model
2. common signs under stress
3. what each pattern is trying to prevent
4. a short note on how secure functioning feels in practice

## Cautions
- Keep the piece explanatory, not diagnostic.
- Avoid making one partner “the anxious one” and the other “the avoidant one” as a frozen identity.
- State that patterns can shift by context, season, and relationship history.

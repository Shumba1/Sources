# Repair Starts With Responsiveness
*Source-scoped candidate derived from psychology-of-sociability-p4--96834b*

## The repair principle
In a strained conversation, most people try to solve the issue before they restore safety.

That usually fails.

A better starting point is **responsiveness**: helping your partner feel that you understand them, value them, and are willing to support them.

Understanding is not agreement.  
Support is not surrender.  
But without responsiveness, repair usually stalls.

## What responsiveness feels like
A responsive partner gives the message:

- “You make sense to me.”
- “What matters to you is not ridiculous to me.”
- “I am still with you while we work this through.”

When people feel that kind of response, they are usually less defensive and more able to stay open.

## What unresponsiveness feels like
A partner often feels unresponsive when they:
- correct too early
- defend too fast
- explain your intention instead of hearing impact
- ask for proof before showing care
- stay technically calm but emotionally absent
- jump to solutions before showing understanding

## The 20-minute responsiveness reset

### Step 1: Slow the pace
One person says:
> “I do want to deal with this. Let’s slow it down enough to do it well.”

### Step 2: One person speaks for two minutes
Not everything. Just the live issue.

Prompt:
> “The part that is hardest for me is...”

### Step 3: The listener reflects before responding
Use this structure:
- what I heard
- what seems to matter most
- what emotion I think is present

Example:
> “What I’m hearing is that when I went quiet, you felt dropped. It sounds like the biggest part wasn’t the silence itself, but that it made you feel unimportant.”

### Step 4: Ask the accuracy question
> “Did I get that mostly right, or did I miss the centre of it?”

No rebuttal yet. Just accuracy.

### Step 5: Name value before problem-solving
> “I can see why this matters to you.”
> “I do not want you carrying that alone.”
> “You are not overreacting to me.”

### Step 6: Ask what support would help now
> “Do you want reassurance, problem-solving, or just company for a minute?”

Do not guess.

## A few sentence stems that help
- “That lands with me.”
- “I can see why you read it that way.”
- “I’m not ready to answer everything yet, but I am here.”
- “There is something valid in what you’re saying.”
- “Let me stay with your experience before I explain mine.”

## What responsiveness is not
It is not:
- instant agreement
- abandoning your own view
- pretending to understand when you do not

If you do not understand, say:
> “I’m trying to get this accurately. Say it again in the simplest way.”

That is more responsive than fake empathy.

## What usually changes first
Do not expect instant resolution.

Often the first visible change is smaller:
- less escalation
- less scorekeeping
- more honesty
- more willingness to hear nuance
- more emotional disclosure

That is progress.

## Bottom line
Couples often think repair begins with the right answer.

More often, repair begins when one person can honestly say:
> “I feel understood enough to stay.”

# We Keep Fighting

When the same conversation keeps turning sharp, flooded, or pointless, the issue is often not just the topic. It is the fight pattern itself.

## Best used when…

- Hard conversations start badly and end worse.
- One of you comes in hot and the other gets defensive or shuts down.
- You both leave discussions feeling more alone than before.
- You keep circling the same issues without real repair.

## Not for…

- abuse, intimidation, or coercive control
- contempt so entrenched that repair attempts are used sarcastically
- situations where one partner uses “communication” language to avoid accountability for betrayal, addiction, or safety issues

## What’s Happening

You are probably not just having “a disagreement.”

You are dropping into a loop.

Common ingredients of that loop:

- criticism instead of complaint
- contempt instead of respect
- defensiveness instead of ownership
- shutdown instead of regulated pause
- bodies getting overloaded
- repair attempts not landing

Once overwhelm is high enough, the conversation often stops being productive.

At that point, winning the point matters more than protecting the bond.

## What Makes It Worse

These patterns usually intensify the loop:

- opening with accusation
- naming character flaws instead of the specific issue
- piling on when the other person is already flooded
- treating a repair attempt as weakness or manipulation
- refusing any influence from the other person
- acting as if full agreement is the only acceptable outcome

A bad opening often poisons the whole conversation.

## Do This First

Lower the heat at entry.

Before you raise the issue, use this structure:

1. name some share of the reality without self-erasure  
2. say what you feel  
3. describe the specific situation  
4. make a positive request

Example:

> I know this is not all on you.  
> I am feeling frustrated and a bit alone about how bedtime has been going.  
> When I handle it by myself three nights in a row, I get resentful.  
> I need us to agree on what support looks like tonight.

That lands better than:

> You never help and I am sick of your selfishness.

## Say This

When the loop has already started:

> I think we are sliding into the same fight pattern again.  
> I do not want to keep proving my case if it means losing each other.  
> Can we slow this down and try to talk about one thing at a time?

If one of you is getting overwhelmed:

> I am not refusing this conversation. I am getting overloaded.  
> I need a real pause so I can come back and do this better.

## Do This Tonight

Name your fight pattern together.

Each person fills in these blanks:

- When I feel ________, I tend to ________.
- When you do that, I tend to ________.
- What I am actually needing in that moment is ________.
- A repair that would help me is ________.

Then agree on:

- one repair phrase
- one pause phrase
- one maximum time for returning after a pause

Example repair phrases:

- “That came out badly. Let me try again.”
- “You are not my enemy right now.”
- “I can hear that you are hurting.”
- “I know I am getting defensive.”

## 7-Day Reset

### Day 1
No character attacks. Only specific situations.

### Day 2
Each person practices one softened start-up.

### Day 3
Use one real repair phrase before the fight gets ugly.

### Day 4
If shutdown starts, call a pause before contempt arrives.

### Day 5
When one of you complains, the other must identify one point they can accept or agree with.

### Day 6
After any rupture, make one explicit repair instead of pretending it did not happen.

### Day 7
Review the week and ask:  
“What usually starts the loop?”  
“What actually helped?”

## Escalate If…

Open a stronger support path if:

- contempt is chronic
- one partner refuses all influence
- shutdown lasts for hours or days as punishment
- every conversation becomes a courtroom
- you cannot attempt repair without mockery or fear

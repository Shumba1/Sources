## 2. Target fit summary

This source is unusually useful for resentment because it focuses on what happens before blowups: invisibility, unmet needs, negative interpretation, and criticism.
It is practical rather than diagnostic. That is a strength.
The material is especially good for couples who are not in high-threat crisis but are steadily poisoning each other with low-grade disappointment.
It should not be used to minimise legitimate structural inequality or chronic neglect. But it is excellent for the common loop where both partners feel unseen and attacked.

## 5. Core content logic

- problem pattern: one or both partners feel they are carrying too much, being misunderstood, or not being cared for, but they are expressing this mostly through criticism or silence.
- likely user state/substate: “I shouldn’t have to ask”; “They never notice what I do”; “I am getting angrier over little things.”
- what is happening: unspoken needs plus negative filtering create a reservoir of resentment that later emerges as criticism, contempt, or sudden overreaction.
- what makes it worse: assuming your partner should mind-read; only noticing what is missing; keeping score privately; starting hard; treating appreciation as unnecessary because effort should be obvious.
- first corrective principle: make yourself understandable before you make your partner wrong.
- first behaviour move: notice one positive effort, thank it out loud, then make one direct, positive request.
- script logic: this module should help users say “I have been getting resentful because I have not been clear about what I need, and I do not want to keep attacking you instead.”
- tonight move: one no-blame conversation that names a need and one thing the partner is doing right.
- reset logic: three-day appreciation streak plus one clear request per day.
- escalation boundary: if there is chronic contempt, mockery, or repeated punishment for having needs, branch outward.

## 6. Claims to preserve

- resentment often grows through invisibility and assumption
- criticism is not an effective need-expression tool
- appreciation shifts climate quickly
- admiration is active, not passive
- requests work better when they describe self rather than attack the partner
- tone at the start matters
